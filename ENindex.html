The Mata Ortiz Technique

The archaeological zone of Paquimé is located in the Municipality of Casas Grandes, Chihuahua, Mexico. It was declared a World Heritage Site by UNESCO in 1998. The artistic expression of painted ceramic began in the 1660-1821 period.

The current Paquimé pots are inspired by the ceramic vessels that were found in the archaeological zone. These pieces, according to archaeologists, were part of the offerings that made up the funeral honors of the Paquimé people.
Five centuries after Paquimé was abandoned, the inhabitants of Mata Ortiz, a small town near Paquimé, recovered the artistic techniques and ceramic style of the Casas Grandes culture. Now, the crafts they create are part of their identity and a way to generate economic resources.

The archaeological pottery of Paquimé is characterized by the presence of small vessels with conventional shapes and in some cases anthropomorphic and zoomorphic shapes that are decorated on their surface with geometric motifs, fretwork, human and animal forms painted in ocher and black colors on white and cream backgrounds. To paint the clay pots, artists use brushes made with human hair and the colors are obtained from local minerals.
